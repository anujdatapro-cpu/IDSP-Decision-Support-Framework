Detailed Model Analysis

Each card shows which parameters influence the model recommendation

Waterfall Model

Linear sequential model where each phase must be completed before the next begins. Best for projects with well-defined requirements.

Parameters that favor this model:

  • Requirement Clarity: High (80%+) - Clear, stable requirements
  • Technology Complexity: Low (below 40%) - Simple technology stack
  • Client Involvement: Low (below 40%) - Minimal client interaction needed
  • Risk Tolerance: Low - Prefers predictable outcomes
  • Project Changes: Minimal expected changes
Pros: Simple to understand, clear milestones, good documentation
Cons: Inflexible, late testing, no working software until end
Best for: Construction projects, government contracts, systems with fixed requirements

Agile Model

Iterative approach with sprints, continuous feedback, and adaptive planning. Perfect for evolving requirements.

Parameters that favor this model:

  • Requirement Clarity: Moderate (40-70%) - Evolving requirements welcome
  • Client Involvement: High (60%+) - Regular feedback needed
  • Team Experience: 5+ years - Self-organizing teams
  • Project Size: Small to Medium - Best for smaller teams
  • Risk Tolerance: Medium - Comfortable with change
Pros: Fast delivery, customer satisfaction, adapts to change
Cons: Less predictability, requires customer availability, minimal documentation
Best for: Web applications, mobile apps, startups, products with evolving features

Spiral Model

Risk-driven model combining prototyping and waterfall. Emphasizes risk analysis at each iteration.

Parameters that favor this model:

  • Technology Complexity: High (60%+) - Complex technical challenges
  • Project Size: Large (70%+) - Enterprise-scale projects
  • Risk Score: High (50%+) - Needs risk mitigation
  • Risk Tolerance: Low - Wants risk control
  • Budget: High - Can afford risk analysis
Pros: Excellent risk management, good for large projects, early prototype
Cons: Expensive, complex management, risk analysis expertise needed
Best for: Large enterprise systems, military projects, high-risk applications

RAD Model

Rapid Application Development focuses on quick prototyping and fast feedback loops.

Parameters that favor this model:

  • Timeline: Short (less than 6 months) - Needs quick delivery
  • Client Involvement: High (70%+) - Close collaboration needed
  • Team Experience: Senior (8+ years) - Experienced developers
  • Project Size: Small to Medium - Modular systems
  • Requirements: Moderately clear - Can be refined
Pros: Fast development, reduced manual coding, reusable components
Cons: Needs strong team, requires modular system, high cost for small projects
Best for: Time-critical applications, UI-heavy systems, component-based projects

Incremental Model

Delivers software in increments, with each release adding more functionality.

Parameters that favor this model:

  • Project Size: Large (70%+) - Big projects need phasing
  • Timeline: Long (12+ months) - Extended delivery period
  • Requirement Clarity: High (70%+) - Core requirements stable
  • Team Size: Medium to Large - Can work on increments
  • Budget: Phased funding - Pay as you go
Pros: Early working software, reduces big-bang risk, easy to manage
Cons: Requires good planning, integration overhead, total cost higher
Best for: Large business systems, ERP implementations, phased rollouts

Prototyping Model

Builds prototypes to understand and refine requirements before final development.

Parameters that favor this model:

  • Requirement Clarity: Low (below 40%) - Needs discovery
  • Client Involvement: High (70%+) - Regular feedback essential
  • Technology Complexity: Medium to High - Need to test feasibility
  • Risk Tolerance: Medium - Accepts refinement
  • Innovation Level: High - New/experimental product
Pros: Clarifies requirements, reduces misunderstandings, user validation
Cons: Can increase cost, user may want finished prototype, extra time
Best for: New product development, UI/UX design, requirements discovery
